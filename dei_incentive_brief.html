Know Your
Rights Zine

Introduced: May 29 Work time: June 5 Gallery walk: June 12 Competency: CCC.2.3
The Big Idea

You're making a zine — a small, handmade guide that someone entering the workforce could actually pick up and use. Your zine covers one workplace situation involving discrimination, bias, or inequity, and what to do about it. This isn't a report. It's a resource. Make something you'd actually want to exist.

Your Task

What Goes In It

A standard one-sheet zine folds into 8 panels. You've got 8 panels to work with — use them however makes sense. Words, drawings, diagrams, whatever communicates best.

Your scenario can be something you've experienced, witnessed, or can imagine happening to someone like you. Pick one situation and go deep.

The 8 Panels

Every zine needs to cover these four things — how you spread them across 8 panels is up to you.

01

The Situation

What happened or could happen — and why it matters. Be specific. Name the dynamic.

02

The Law

What legal protections apply — federal, state, or local. Which law covers this, and who does it protect?

03

The Steps

What to do: how to document, who to tell, when to escalate. A practical sequence someone could follow.

04

Standing Up

What allyship looks like here — for yourself or for a coworker. What does actually doing something look like?

Format

Making It

Medium
One sheet of paper, folded into 8 panels
Tools
Markers, colored pencils, pens — provided June 5
Style
Your call. Words, drawings, diagrams, collage-style
There is no single right way to make a zine. The goal is something readable, specific, and yours. It should take someone 2–4 minutes to read through. If it could help a person who looks like you navigate something real — you're on the right track.
